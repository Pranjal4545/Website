Chapter 1: Introduction to Computer Science

Introduction

Computer is an electronic device derived from the Latin word "computere," which means to calculate. It accepts raw facts and figures as input through input devices, processes it according to the user's requirements, stores it before and after processing, and produces meaningful information as output through output devices. Generally, a computer works on the IPO (Input-Process-Output) cycle and follows the GIGO (Garbage In, Garbage Out) principle, meaning the output is always based on the input supplied by the user. Some characteristics/features of computers:

Characteristics of Computers

  • Accuracy: Computers are accurate machines; results produced by computers are 100% accurate, as errors arise from human input.
  • Speed: Computers work at tremendously high speeds, measured in milliseconds, microseconds, picoseconds, and nanoseconds.
  • Storage: Computers store data in high volumes measured in Megabytes (MB), Gigabytes (GB), Terabytes (TB), and Petabytes (PB).
  • Versatility: Computers are designed to serve multiple purposes and can be used for various tasks.
  • Diligence: Computers donâ€™t get tired and can perform tasks continuously until completed.
  • Automatic: Computers automatically perform tasks once instructions are given.

Applications of Computers

The uses of computers are increasing day by day across various fields:

  • Education: Used in teaching and learning processes.
  • Communication: Facilitates communication through email, chat, and online conferencing.
  • Business: Used for generating invoices, maintaining stock, and e-commerce.
  • Engineering and Designing: Used for complex computations in design and research.
  • Science and Research, Military, Industry, Medicine, Robotics, Transportation.

Capabilities and Limitations of Computers

Capabilities (Advantages):
  • Handle large volumes of data.
  • Perform millions of instructions rapidly and accurately.
  • Provide faster and cheaper communication.
  • Handle complex situations where human intervention is difficult.
Limitations (Disadvantages):
  • Dull machine; cannot make decisions independently.
  • Requires electricity or battery to operate.
  • Can affect eyesight with prolonged use.
  • Needs various peripheral devices and software to function.
  • Cannot think, learn, or react like humans.

Generations of Computers

The history of computers dates back to the mechanical era, evolving through various generations:

  1. First Generation (1946-1958): Used vacuum tubes. Example: ENIAC, UNIVAC.
  2. Second Generation (1959-1964): Used transistors. Example: IBM 1401.
  3. Third Generation (1965-1974): Used Integrated Circuits. Example: IBM 360.
  4. Fourth Generation (1975-1990): Used VLSI technology. Example: IBM PC.
  5. Fifth Generation (1990-onwards): Uses ULSI and artificial intelligence.

Introduction to Computer System

A computer system is a combination of hardware, software, and peripherals organized to run smoothly. It operates on the IPO cycle, and various components must work together to convert raw data into meaningful information.

Components of a Computer System

  • Hardware (Input, Output, Processing, Storage)
  • Software
  • Data/Information
  • Procedure
  • People/User
  • Communication
